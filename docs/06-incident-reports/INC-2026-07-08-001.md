# Incident Report: INC-2026-07-08-001
 
---
 
## 1. Incident Summary
 
On July 8, 2026 at approximately 15:12 CEST, an unusually high alert volume was observed originating from ws-dev-02 (10.10.20.20) during routine review of the SOC L1 dashboard. Initial investigation identified a coordinated intrusion originating from 10.10.66.10 in the Adversary DMZ (VLAN 66), targeting workstations in the Development VLAN (VLAN 20).
 
The observed activity included a network scan, an SSH brute force attempt against user `arodriguez` on ws-dev-02, and subsequent successful authentication from the same source IP. Follow-on activity indicated lateral movement to a second host (WS-DEV-01) and modification of persistence-relevant files on both workstations.
 
The incident was declared at 16:45 CEST after correlation of the observed events confirmed a real intrusion. Containment actions were initiated at 17:00 CEST and completed at 17:15 CEST. Full forensic investigation and root cause analysis were escalated to L2 for follow-up.

The dominant characteristic of the alert profile was **high volume of low-severity events**. The pfSense block events and SSH authentication failures generated the majority of the volume but did not individually indicate a serious incident. Manual correlation was required to reconstruct the attack pattern from these signals.

## 5. Immediate Actions Taken
 
The following actions were performed by SOC L1 to contain the incident during the shift.
 
- pfSense firewall rule deployed blocking bidirectional traffic between VLAN 20 (Dev) and VLAN 66 (Adversary DMZ). Rule verified active via pfSense management interface.
- Local user account `arodriguez` on ws-dev-02 disabled using `passwd -l`. Verified with `passwd -S arodriguez`.
- Local user account `khernandez` on WS-DEV-01 disabled using `passwd -l`. Verified with `passwd -S khernandez`.
- Active SSH sessions on ws-dev-02 and WS-DEV-01 terminated. No active sessions remained for the affected users after verification with `who` and `w`.
- Snapshot of both affected hosts requested from infrastructure team before any host modifications, preserving state for forensic investigation.
- Incident escalated to SOC L2 with handoff notes attached.
Note: forensic investigation, credential rotation, persistence artefact removal, and root cause analysis were escalated to L2 and are not part of the L1 scope for this report.

## 6. Lessons Learned
 
The following observations were noted during the shift for inclusion in the post-incident review meeting.
 
- The incident was identified through manual review of alert volume rather than through a targeted alert. The 40,000+ low-severity alerts generated during the attack did not trigger any high-severity notification on the L1 dashboard. Detection depended on the analyst noticing an unusual volume pattern.
- The correlation between the SSH authentication failure burst and the subsequent authentication success from the same source IP was identified manually. No rule was in place to automatically flag this pattern, which is a well-known indicator of successful brute force compromise.
- Alert triage across multiple event sources (pfSense blocks, SSH failures, SSH successes) required manual pivoting between different searches in the Wazuh dashboard. A consolidated view or a correlated alert would have significantly reduced triage time.
- Containment actions were straightforward and completed within 15 minutes of incident declaration. The pfSense-based network isolation was effective for stopping ongoing communication with the attacker source.

## 7. Handoff Notes
 
The following items are escalated to L2 for follow-up investigation and remediation.
 
**Forensic investigation required:**
- Full examination of ws-dev-02 for credential harvest activity (files potentially accessed by the attacker: `.env`, `notes.txt`, `.ssh/` contents).
- Full examination of both hosts for persistence artefacts (cron entries, authorized_keys modifications, systemd units, additional user accounts).
- Verification of the source of the SSH key used for lateral movement to WS-DEV-01.
- Credential rotation for all accounts and secrets referenced in files accessed by the attacker.
  
**Detection engineering follow-up:**
- Development of custom Wazuh detection rules recommended to address the detection gaps observed during this incident, specifically: aggregation of high-volume pfSense block events into port scan alerts, aggregation of SSH authentication failures into brute force alerts, compound correlation of brute force followed by successful login, and file access monitoring for credential-bearing paths and persistence-relevant configuration files.
- Custom rule design and deployment to be documented under `docs/05-detection-rules/` and validated against a replay of this incident.
  
**Post-incident review meeting:**
- L1 timeline (this document) contributed as evidence.
- Full root cause analysis, impact assessment, and recommendation prioritisation to be completed by L2 and detection engineering leads.
